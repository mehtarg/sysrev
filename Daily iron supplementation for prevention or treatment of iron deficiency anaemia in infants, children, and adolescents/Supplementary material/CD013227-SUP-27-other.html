Trial author

Zhang 2013

Brief name

Multiple micronutrient supplementation (MMS) trial to reduce anemia and anxiety in rural Chinese schoolchildren

Recipient

Fourth-grade elementary school students (mean age ~10–12 years) from 54 schools in 8 of the poorest counties in Shaanxi Province, China

Why

To assess whether daily MMS improves hemoglobin concentration and mental health (anxiety), addressing poor nutrition, anemia, and psychological stress in rural Chinese children

What (materials)

- MMS tablet containing 5 mg iron (ferrous sulfate) and 20 additional vitamins and minerals (formulation not fully detailed in main text; provided in supplement)
- No placebo given to control group

What (procedures)

- Cluster-randomized controlled trial over 5 months
- MMS administered daily at school by teachers during homeroom after lunch
- Students received two tablets on Friday to take home for weekend consumption
- Mental Health Test (MHT) and hemoglobin levels measured at baseline and endline
- No MMS during 3.5-week winter break
- Anxiety measured using validated MHT (100 yes/no items)

Who provided

Homeroom teachers distributed tablets to students and were paid 100 yuan/month for participation; MMS sourced commercially (available in cities but not in study region)

How (mode of delivery; individual or group)

Group-based administration in classrooms under teacher supervision

Where

54 elementary schools in rural Shaanxi Province, China (boarding and day students)

When and how much

- One MMS tablet per day for 5 months (excluding weekends and winter break)
- Total supplementation period: ~36 weeks
- MMS delivery every 6 weeks by research team

Tailoring

n/a

Modification of intervention throughout the trial

n/a

Strategies to improve or maintain intervention fidelity

- Homeroom teachers compensated financially
- Regular unannounced visits by researchers to ensure compliance
- Control schools visited during same intervals to avoid Hawthorne effect
- Participants blinded to hypothesis; control schools unaware of intervention arm

Extent of intervention fidelity

Nearly 100% compliance based on field team visits and teacher reporting; endline data collected from 85% of baseline sample

What (procedures)

"Information on sociodemographic characteristics of the families and the anthropometric measurements of their infants was retrieved from personal files at the Well Child Unit...

Blood count analysis was conducted by an ABX pentra 120-HORIBA device. Serum ferritin was determined by a modular analytic E 170 device, using the electro-chemiluminescence immunoassay method...

Infants in both groups continued to receive vitamin D supplementation. Mothers of the infants in the control group were told not to give any supplementation besides vitamin D. Trial author

Moafi 2011

Brief name

Effect of iron supplementation on educational achievement in university students with iron deficiency without anemia (IDWA)

Recipient

Female university students (aged 18–26) with IDWA (normal hemoglobin >12 g/dL and low ferritin <20 ng/dL) at the Faculty of Science, University of Isfahan

Why

To assess whether correcting IDWA in female students improves educational performance, considering the high prevalence of IDWA and its potential impact on brain function and academic achievement

What (materials)

- Iron sulfate tablets containing 50 mg elemental iron (intervention group)
- Placebo pills (prepared at School of Pharmacy, Isfahan University of Medical Sciences) (control group)

What (procedures)

- Randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial over 6 weeks
- 3 courses of treatment: participants took 2 pills daily (iron or placebo)
- Academic performance evaluated before and after supplementation using GPA and university ranking
- Ferritin measured pre- and post-intervention using EDTA and coagulated blood samples
- Monitored side effects (e.g., GI issues) and adherence

Who provided

Supplements administered via pre-labeled pill packs; prepared and blinded by Isfahan University of Medical Sciences pharmacy faculty

How (mode of delivery; individual or group)

Individually administered at home, twice daily

Where

University of Isfahan, Isfahan, Iran

When and how much

- 6 weeks total
- 50 mg elemental iron, 2× daily (total: 100 mg/day)
- Participants took 86 pills over the 6-week period

Tailoring

n/a

Modification of intervention throughout the trial

Participants with side effects or other personal issues (e.g., marriage, illness) were excluded mid-trial; otherwise, protocol unchanged

Strategies to improve or maintain intervention fidelity

- Participants were monitored biweekly
- Pill packs labeled with names
- Side effects and pill usage tracked every 2 weeks
- Double-blinding maintained (except principal investigators)

Extent of intervention fidelity

- 65 of 72 participants completed the full intervention (32 in case, 33 in placebo)
- One dropout in case group due to intolerance
- Academic scores and rankings improved significantly in the case group only

Trial author

Chang 2010

Brief name

Iron and zinc supplementation: separate vs combined delivery to reduce diarrhea and improve anemia

Recipient

Children aged 6–18 months in rural Bangladesh (Mirzapur)

Why

To evaluate the clinical effectiveness and interaction of iron and zinc when delivered separately or in combination, particularly regarding diarrhea, anemia, and hospitalization outcomes

What (materials)

Tablets (dissolvable in water), provided as:
- Iron/folic acid: 12.5 mg iron + 50 IU folic acid
- Zinc: 10 mg
- Placebo: visually identical, same taste
Manufactured by Nutriset S.A.S (France)

What (procedures)

- Randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled factorial trial
- 5 intervention arms: placebo, zinc alone, iron alone, combined iron+zinc, iron and zinc given separately
- Supplements reconstituted daily in 5 mL water and given on an empty stomach
- Weekly health follow-up by Village Health Workers (VHWs); blood sampling and clinical outcome measurements (e.g., hemoglobin, transferrin receptor, serum zinc)

Who provided

Village Health Workers (VHWs) trained and supervised supplement delivery and health assessments

How (mode of delivery; individual or group)

Individually administered at home, supervised by VHWs

Where

Community setting in rural Mirzapur, Bangladesh

When and how much

- Daily supplementation for 6 months
- Zinc group: average 5 mg/day (10 mg every other day)
- Iron group: average 6.25 mg/day (12.5 mg every other day)
- Combined/separate groups: same daily doses as above
- Children <12 months received half tablets

Tailoring

Doses halved for children <12 months (2.5 mg zinc, 3.125 mg iron/day)

Modification of intervention throughout the trial

- Minor deviations (9 cases received wrong supplements; 2 had mix-ups longer than 1 week but were included in intention-to-treat analysis)
- Supplementation extended for some children to complete blood draws

Strategies to improve or maintain intervention fidelity

- Rigorous VHW training (initial + refresher)
- Weekly home visits; supplement use and health status recorded
- Supervisors audited VHW work weekly and validated data
- Blinded randomization and analysis

Extent of intervention fidelity

- 96.5% of participants followed ≥90% of eligible time
- Adherence: 81.9% overall; 92.2% when children were home
- Vomiting rare (<1%); slightly higher in combined iron+zinc group
- Minimal loss to follow-up and protocol violations

Brief name

Effect of early iron supplementation of breastfed infants

Recipient

"Normal infants who were exclusively breastfed at 1 mo of age and whose mothers intended to breastfeed to at least 5.5 mo of age"

Why

"Iron supplementation starting at an early age may prevent iron deficiency...The study assessed the effect of early iron supplementation of breastfed infants and tested the hypothesis that iron supplementation enhances iron status."

What (materials)

—

What (procedures)

"Blood was obtained by heel stick at enrollment, 4 mo (112 d), 5.5 mo (168 d), 7.5 mo(224 d), 9 mo (280 d), 12 mo, 15 mo, and 18 mo. Blood was used for determination of plasma concentrations of ferritin (PF),soluble transferrin receptor (sTfR), concentration of hemoglobin, and red cell indices. C-reactive protein (CRP) was measured in the first 35 subjects but measurement was discontinued when it was found not to be useful in classifying elevated PF values...

Visits occurred within the specified tolerance limits of 64 d (for infants aged 1–9mo) and 67 d (for infants aged 12–18 mo), respectively, with the exception of 14 visits (5 between 1 and 9 mo and 9 between 12 and 18 mo) that occurred outside these limits. At each visit, weight and length were measured using established methods(20) by 2 individuals (out of a pool of 5) trained in anthropometric methods. Length was measured by 2 individuals who each made separate measurements in alternating positions. If their measurements did not agree to 4 mm, the measurements were repeated until agreement was reached. The average of the 2 measurements was used. Scales were calibrated at regular intervals. Before each visit, parents completed a feeding questionnaire and a stool and behavior questionnaire. The feeding questionnaire asked whether the infant was fed formula or complementary foods and how much of these the infant typically consumed. In the stool questionnaire the parents recorded for each of the 4 preceding days the color, number, and consistency of stools as well as feeding-related behaviors such as spitting up, fussiness, and gas. The questionnaires were checked at the time of the visit for completeness by the nursing staff, and any ambiguities were clarified...

Approximately 1 mL of blood was collected into heparin-treated tubes. Hemoglobin concentration and red cell indices [mean corpuscular volume (MCV) and red cell distribution width (RDW)] were determined immediately on whole blood by using a Coulter AcT diff Hematology Analyzer (Coulter Corporation, Irving, TX). The blood was then centrifuged, and plasma was used to measure PF concentration(by radioimmunoassay using the Quantimune kit; Bio-Rad Laboratories, Hercules, CA); sTfR concentration was measured by using enzyme immunoassay (Ramco kit, catalog number TF-94; Houston, TX) kit. CRP concentration was determined by a 2-sided enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay developed in our laboratory...

Percentiles of weight and length were calculated on the basis of the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention 2000 growth references [3]."

Trial author

Hettiarachchi 2008

Brief name

"The efficacy of micronutrient supplementation in reducing the prevalence of anaemia and deficiencies of zinc and iron among adolescents in Sri Lanka"

Recipient

"Children 12–16 years of age attending schools in the Galle District...with Hb level 80 g/l "

Why

"To determine the effectiveness of combined iron and zinc over the iron or zinc-only supplementation in correcting deficiency and possible interactive effects in a group of adolescent school children."

What (materials)

"The principals of the schools and the class teachers were briefed about the research project and then the subjects and their parents were informed by a letter, which described in detail the procedures involved in the study."

What (procedures)

Screening: "A sample of venous blood (3 ml) was drawn for the initial assessment of micronutrient status. An aliquot of 200  l of whole blood was pipetted onto a filter paper for measurement of Hb, and rest of the blood was collected in acid-washed centrifuge tubes, and transferred to the laboratory, where serum was separated by centrifugation and kept frozen at −20°C until SZn and SF analyses were carried out. The dried filter paper was placed in a test tube containing Drabkin's solution for the Hb measurement on the following day...

All the study subjects were treated for parasites by giving mebendazole (500 mg) as a single oral dose (mass-treatment) approximately 2 weeks before the start of the study...

The final assessment was carried out 1 week after the end of the intervention period. During this assessment, anthropometric measurements were taken, a medical examination was carried out and 3 ml of venous blood was drawn for determination of Hb, SZn and SF concentrations...

Hb concentration was measured photometrically using the cyanmethaemoglobin method at the Nutrition Research Laboratory, Faculty of Medicine, Galle. SF was measured by immunoradiometric assay at the Nuclear Medicine Unit, Faculty of Medicine, Galle using reagents supplied by NETRIA, England. SZn was analysed at the Industrial Technology Institute (ITI), Colombo by flame atomic absorption spectrophotometry (f-AAS)."

Who provided

"The subjects received capsules...from their class teacher."

How (mode of delivery; individual or group)

"The subjects received capsules daily... from their class teacher...each morning at the time daily attendance was taken."

Where

"schools in the Galle District...urban and rural children in equal proportions"

When and how much

"Four groups of children were supplemented with two capsules per day containing either iron (50 mg/day) in the form of ferrous fumarate, zinc (14 mg/day) in the form of zinc sulphate, combined (iron+zinc) or placebo made of anhydrous lactose...

The subjects received capsules daily on school days for a period of 24 weeks from their class teacher. The capsules were consumed each morning at the time daily attendance was taken."

Tailoring

"A letter was given to their family physicians requesting notification if they wished to prescribe additional micronutrient supplements during the study period."

Modification of intervention throughout the trial

—

Strategies to improve or maintain intervention fidelity

"Teachers were instructed to ensure that their students consumed the capsules. Capsules were provided to the class teachers by the investigators every 2 weeks; the teachers were asked to sign a checklist when the doses were given. These checklists were randomly checked for accuracy and compliance."

Extent of intervention fidelity

"Of the children recruited, 774 completed the 24-week study protocol. A total of 47 (5.7%) children were dropped for various reasons: withdrawal from the study (n=23), refusal to give blood after supplementation (n=7), and absence on the day of the post-supplementation blood collection (n=17)."
