Amirsasan 2024

Brief name

The effect of sumac powder along with ferrous sulfate on blood markers and oxidative stress in young athletic girls with NAID

Recipient

Teenage female volleyball players (aged 15–18) with non-anemic iron deficiency (serum ferritin <40 ng/mL)

Why

Sumac was hypothesized to improve iron metabolism by reducing inflammation (hs-CRP) and oxidative stress, enhancing the effects of iron supplementation in athletes

What (materials)

Capsules containing:
- Ferrous sulfate (50 mg elemental iron)
- Sumac powder (1 g)
- Placebo (rice flour, 1 g)

What (procedures)
  • Pre- and post-tests including: fitness tests (agility, anaerobic and aerobic power)
  • Blood collection for ferritin, Hb, Hct, MCV, hs-CRP, MDA, TAC
  • 6 weeks of supplementation and training (2 volleyball + 2 fitness sessions/week)
  • 24-h food recall and dietary standardization
  • Blood samples collected at both phases and analyzed with standard clinical kits

Who provided

n/a

How (mode of delivery; individual or group)

Individual capsules taken daily

Where

University of Tabriz, Iran; training and testing in the university sports hall

When and how much

Daily for 6 weeks:
- 50 mg elemental iron (ferrous sulfate capsule)
- 1 g sumac powder (capsule) in combo group
- 1 g rice flour (placebo group)

Tailoring

Comprehensive dietary guidance was given to avoid foods inhibiting or enhancing iron absorption and standardize intake

Modification of intervention throughout the trial

n/a

Strategies to improve or maintain intervention fidelity

Subjects excluded if they missed >2 training sessions or irregularly consumed supplements

Extent of intervention fidelity

Monitored via exclusion criteria (e.g., supplement adherence, training attendance); however, specific adherence rates not reported

Brief name

Effect of six-week aerobic training, calcium and iron supplementation on resting calcium levels among school-level athletes

Recipient

60 male school-level athletes aged 12–19 years from a state sports hostel in Himachal Pradesh

Why

To determine if aerobic training combined with supplementation (calcium, iron, combo, placebo) significantly affects resting calcium levels in athletes, which may decrease due to intense training and result in mineral deficiencies

What (materials)

- Iron group: 10 mg Ferrous Sulphate + 1.5 mg Folic Acid/day

What (procedures)

- Pre-, mid-, and post-test blood samples collected (Day 0, 21, 42)
- Resting blood samples analyzed using automated biochemistry analyzer (XL-1000)
- Six weeks of aerobic training applied to all groups
- Statistical analysis via repeated measures ANOVA and post hoc testing using SPSS v23

Who provided

n/a

Individually administered daily oral supplementation

Where

Training at State Sports Hostel, Una, Himachal Pradesh; biochemical tests at IGMC Shimla biochemistry lab

When and how much

Daily for 6 weeks:
- Calcium: 1000 mg/day
- Iron: 10 mg Ferrous Sulphate + 1.5 mg Folic Acid/day
- Combo: Calcium in morning + Iron in evening
- Placebo: 100 mg sugar-coated ascorbate/day

Tailoring

n/a

n/a

All participants were informed of procedures and gave written consent; trained technician conducted sample collection; random sampling ensured balanced group assignment

Extent of intervention fidelity

n/a

Trial author

Long 2024

Brief name

Randomized trial of physical activity (PA) and multi-micronutrient supplementation (MMNS) to improve body composition and micronutrient status among South African schoolchildren

Recipient

Schoolchildren aged 6–12 years in public schools in peri-urban Gqeberha, Eastern Cape Province, South Africa

Why

To assess whether PA, MMNS, or their combination improves fat mass (FM), fat-free mass (FFM), and micronutrient status, addressing rising childhood obesity and micronutrient deficiencies in urbanizing African contexts

What (materials)

- MMNS supplement: modified MixMe™ powder containing micronutrients (e.g., zinc, vitamin D, β-carotene instead of vitamin A)
- Placebo powder: identical in packaging and taste
- Teaching materials: KaziKidz PA curriculum for teachers
- Bioimpedance analyzer (Tanita MC-580), ELISA kits for biomarkers

What (procedures)

- Cluster RCT across 4 intervention arms: PA only, MMNS only, PA+MMNS, and placebo
- Physical measurements and blood samples at 3 time points (baseline, 9 months, 28 months)
- Micronutrient assessments: retinol-binding protein, vitamin D, zinc, serum transferrin receptor (sTfR)
- Body composition via bioelectrical impedance analysis
- Data analyzed via latent growth curve modeling (LGCM)

Who provided

- MMNS and placebo administered by classroom teachers
- PA intervention supported by trained physical education staff guiding teachers using KaziKidz lesson plans

How (mode of delivery; individual or group)

Group-based: class-level randomization and implementation of PA lessons; individual daily supplement administration in class

Where

Public primary schools in peri-urban areas of Gqeberha, Eastern Cape, South Africa

When and how much

- PA: daily short activity breaks, 1× weekly 45–60 min PE lesson, and 1× weekly 45–60 min dance session
- MMNS: daily supplement (dosage per MixMe™ spec; includes 4,500 µg β-carotene)
- Duration: intervention ran from 2019 to 2021 (with assessments at ~9 months and ~28 months)

Tailoring

- Supplement formulation modified to include β-carotene instead of vitamin A to target adiposity
- PA sessions adapted via the KaziKidz curriculum to the local school environment

Modification of intervention throughout the trial

COVID-19 pandemic caused school closures and disrupted continuous delivery and follow-up; adjustments made post-pandemic to resume follow-up in 2021

Strategies to improve or maintain intervention fidelity

- Teachers blinded to allocation
- Study staff monitored lesson delivery and guided teachers in PA intervention
- Supplements administered consistently during school days (5×/week)

Extent of intervention fidelity

Fidelity partially affected by COVID-19 disruptions; initial implementation robust, but follow-up sample dropped from 1,304 (baseline) to 526 (final assessment)

Svensson 2024

Brief name

Effect of low-dose iron supplementation (1 mg/kg/day) on early development in breastfed infants

Recipient

Healthy singleton infants born at term (>2500 g) who were exclusively or predominantly breastfed (>50%) at 4 months and not anemic (Hb >10.5 g/dL)

Why

To evaluate whether daily iron supplementation from 4 to 9 months improves psychomotor development and reduces risk of iron deficiency or anemia at 12 months in a setting of low risk for iron deficiency

What (materials)

- Sachets with micronized microencapsulated ferric pyrophosphate (iron) mixed with maltodextrin (for intervention group)
- Identical sachets with maltodextrin only (placebo group)

What (procedures)

- Randomized, placebo-controlled, double-blind trial
- Daily supplement from 4 to 9 months of age
- Development assessed via Bayley Scales of Infant and Toddler Development III at 12, 24, and 36 months
- Blood samples taken at 4 and 12 months for Hb, ferritin, and CRP
- Adverse events tracked (e.g., GI symptoms); noncompliance defined as returning ≥25% of sachets

Who provided

Caregivers administered the daily sachet mixed in 10 mL of lukewarm water or breast milk

How (mode of delivery; individual or group)

Individually administered at home by caregivers

Where

Study conducted in outpatient settings in Poland and Sweden; supplement given at home

When and how much

Once daily from age 4 to 9 months (5 months duration)
- Dosage adjusted by weight:
- <7 kg: 7 mg/day
- 7–10 kg: 10 mg/day
- >10 kg: 15 mg/day

Tailoring

Dosage tailored based on infant's weight to approximate 1 mg/kg/day

Modification of intervention throughout the trial

Recruitment modified mid-study: halted in Poland and completed in Sweden due to low recruitment pace

Strategies to improve or maintain intervention fidelity

- Blinded allocation with coded sachets
- Sachet return used to monitor compliance
- Compliance monitored; ≥25% returned sachets indicated noncompliance

Extent of intervention fidelity

67.6% compliance in iron group, 64.5% in placebo; 90% follow-up at primary outcome (12 months); data from returned sachets assessed

Csulak 2023

Brief name

Iron supplementation trial in young male basketball players with ferritin <100 μg/L

Recipient

Male basketball players aged <19 years with non-anemic iron deficiency (ferritin <100 μg/L), training ~20 h/week in a youth academy

Why

To evaluate whether a higher ferritin cut-off (100 μg/L) is appropriate to identify iron deficiency without anemia (IDNA), and if 3-month iron supplementation improves ferritin levels and/or performance in young athletes

What (materials)

- Intervention group: Sorbifer durules tablets (320 mg ferrous sulfate) containing 100 mg elemental iron (II) + 60 mg ascorbic acid
- Placebo group: Tablets containing 50 mg vitamin C

What (procedures)

- Randomized, single-blind, placebo-controlled trial over 3 months
- Pre- and post-testing: medical history, ECG, echocardiography, laboratory tests (iron panel), body composition (InBody 770), cardiopulmonary exercise testing (CPET)
- Nutrition assessed via food frequency questionnaire (FFQ) and dietary counseling
- Iron and placebo tablets administered every second day, 2× daily

Who provided

Tablets assigned by a dietitian; physicians remained blinded during the study

How (mode of delivery; individual or group)

Individually administered tablets every second day

Where

Hungary – Semmelweis University and Honvéd Basketball Academy

When and how much

- Duration: 3 months
- Dose: One tablet twice daily every second day

Tailoring

Stratified randomization by age group (U15, U16, U18, U19); iron and placebo administered accordingly; dietary advice provided to avoid additional supplementation

Modification of intervention throughout the trial

n/a

Strategies to improve or maintain intervention fidelity

- Single-blind design (physicians blinded)
- Dietary counseling and FFQ to monitor intake
- Compliance ensured via stratified and simple randomization; dietitian managed supplementation adherence

Extent of intervention fidelity

- 51 athletes randomized (26 iron, 25 placebo)
- No dropouts reported; 3-month follow-up completed
- Significant increase in ferritin in the iron group; only 15% reached >100 μg/L; no performance improvement detected

Trial author

Varea 2023

Brief name

Effectiveness of iron supplementation in preventing iron deficiency anaemia in infants

Recipient

3-month-old infants

Why

"There is evidence that adherence to daily preventive supplementation is low.9,10 In the 1990s, weekly administration was proposed as an alternative for the prevention of anemia. The rationale for such proposal was that intestinal cells are renewed every 5–6 days and are limited in their ability to absorb iron. In contrast, intermittent iron administration would expose only new epithelial cells to this nutrient and improve absorption efficiency.11 However, most of these studies were focused on pregnant women and children older than 1 year; the evidence in infants was scant"

What (materials)

—

What (procedures)

"Before and after the intervention, a blood sample (3 mL) was collected from the infants, and the following hematological variables were measured: hemoglobin (Hb), mean corpuscular volume (MCV), mean corpuscular hemoglobin (MCH), and mean corpuscular hemoglobin concentration (MCHC), using a Coulter counter (Pentra XLR Horiba Medical, France); ferritin, by enzyme immunoassay (Access-Beckman Coulter, Fullerton, USA); and C-reactive protein (CRP), by turbidimetry (BT 3000 Plus Wiener Autoanalyzer). Anemia was defined as follows: Hb < 9.5 g/dL at 3 months of age and < 11.0 g/dL at 6 months of age, ID: ferritin < 12 ng/mL, and IDA: Hb < 11 g/dL and ferritin < 12 ng/mL.15 A possible inflammatory state was considered if CRP was ≥ 5 mg/L, in which case ID was established if ferritin was < 30 ng/mL. At study initiation, the infant’s and their family’s sociodemographic data were collected. Weight and height were measured on a monthly basis. Z-scores for weight-for-age (W-A), height-for- age (H-A), and weight-for-height (W-H) were developed according to the tables proposed by the World Health Organization.17

In each health checkup, adverse events (refusal to take it, constipation, vomiting, diarrhea and/or abdominal pain) and adherence to supplementation were recorded, taking into account the information provided by the adult in charge. Adherence was considered high if the infant complied with more than 80% of the prescription, moderate if it was between 50% and 80%, and low if it was less than 50%"

Who provided

—

How (mode of delivery; individual or group)

Participants in the iron group received the intervention in the form of an oral solution containing ferrous sulphate

Where

Home-based

When and how much

1 mg/ kg/ day on a daily basis with a maximum of 40 mg /d for 3 months

Tailoring

"The dosage and administration of the drug were accompanied by instructions and a dosage recording calendar that was adjusted monthly by the pediatrician"

Modification of intervention throughout the trial

—

Strategies to improve or maintain intervention fidelity

—

Extent of intervention fidelity

Adherence to the intervention was assessed; "Regarding adherence to supplementation, 50.6% of the infants with the daily intervention and 57.1% with the weekly intervention had a high level of adherence, with no significant differences between them."

Effect of iron supplementaion in children with ADHD and ID

Recipient

"children/adolescents with ADHD and iron deficiency"

Why

"there have been no studies investigating the effect of iron supplementation in children with ADHD and iron deficiency who are concomitantly on stimulant medication"

What (materials)

—

What (procedures)

"Complete blood count and measurement of serum ferritin levels, as well as serum iron levels and total iron-binding capacity...

participants in both the ferrous and placebo groups were assessed for ADHD symptoms by parents and teachers using 2 parts of the Vanderbilt Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder Diagnostic Rating Scales. [1, 2] The first part is an 18-item scale encompassing 18 symptoms of DSM-IV criteria for ADHD: the first 9 items include an inattention subscale and the other 9 items a hyperactivity-impulsivity subscale. Each item can be scored on a scale of 0 to 3 (0, never; 1, oc-casionally; 2, often; and 3, very often), with a higher score denoting more severe ADHD. The second part consists of 8 items evaluating the child’s academic and behavioral performance. Each item in the second part can be scored on a scale of 1 to 5 (1, excellent; 2, above average; 3, average; 4, somewhat of a problem; and 5, problematic), with a higher score indicating a worse performance. One parent and 1 teacher Vanderbilt Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder Rating Scale was obtained per child at each study time point...

Participants were interviewed for symptoms of rest-less legs syndrome (RLS) at the initial assessment using 2012 Revised International Restless Legs Syndrome Study Group Diagnostic Criteria for RLS.[3]...

Parents in both groups were interviewed regarding their child's potential side effects, including abdominal pain, nausea, vomiting, constipation, and diarrhea at the end of this study."

Tailoring

Modification of intervention throughout the trial

Group-based administration in school, under daily supervision by teachers

Where

8 primary schools in 2 rural districts of Hai Phong City, Vietnam

When and how much

- One capsule per school day (5×/week for 22 weeks = ~110 days)
- Mean intakes during study: ~1070 mg of iron and zinc, ~42,800 µg vitamin A

Tailoring

Supplement modified from national standard to include 10 mg zinc instead of 4.1 mg

Modification of intervention throughout the trial

n/a

- Unused blister packs collected and checked
- Identical packaging for blinding

Extent of intervention fidelity

High adherence: mean of 107 capsules consumed (95.7% compliance); no loss to follow-up
